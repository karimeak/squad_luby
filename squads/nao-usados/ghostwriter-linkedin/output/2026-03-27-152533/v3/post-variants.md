## Variante A — Contrarian + Trust Crisis

Something I keep seeing with my clients right now:

They're rushing to adopt AI coding tools.

And they're getting exactly what they asked for. More code. Faster.

But here's what most people overlook.

84% of developers now use AI to write code. Yet 46% don't trust the results. And independent research shows AI-generated code produces 1.7x more issues than human-written code.

Read that again.

The companies adopting fastest are also creating the most technical debt.

My clients always ask me: "Should we use AI for development?"

The better question is: "Do we have the infrastructure to manage what AI produces?"

Because the real cost isn't the tool. It's what happens after:

1. Security vulnerabilities increase 23.7% with heavy AI code reliance
2. Code volume grows faster than your team's ability to review it
3. Speed gains disappear when you're fixing bugs that a human wouldn't have written
4. Governance frameworks that didn't exist 2 years ago are now table stakes
5. The teams winning aren't coding faster. They're reviewing smarter.

The pattern is clear.

AI development tools aren't a productivity play. They're a risk management play.

The companies that figure this out first will spend less fixing what AI broke and more building what AI enabled.

## Variante B — Data-driven + Case Study

79% faster time to market.

That's what Rakuten achieved when they used AI to implement a complex infrastructure task. Seven hours. Zero human code during execution.

Something I keep seeing: every executive reads that headline and thinks, "We need this everywhere."

But here's the number nobody talks about.

Anthropic's own engineers measured their productivity gains at +50%. Impressive, right?

Until you look closer.

That +50% only showed up in three specific areas: debugging, code understanding, and feature implementation. Not in design. Not in planning. Not in architecture.

The real productivity story isn't +50% across the board.

It's +300% on the right bottleneck. And +0% on everything else.

What most people overlook is that targeting matters more than adoption.

The companies I work with that see real returns from AI development aren't the ones deploying it everywhere. They're the ones who figured out exactly where it works:

1. Scoped, well-defined tasks with clear acceptance criteria
2. Code reviews and debugging where pattern matching beats human speed
3. Legacy modernization where reading millions of lines isn't humanly efficient
4. Prototyping where speed to first version matters more than perfection
5. Infrastructure tasks where precision and consistency beat creativity

My clients always ask me: "How do we get the 79% improvement?"

The honest answer: you probably won't. Not on most things.

But on the right things, the impact is real and measurable.

Which bottleneck in your development process would benefit most from AI acceleration?
